01 · Preamble

This Product Requirements Document specifies the Common Information System for RegenHub, LCA, a Colorado public benefit limited cooperative association. The system's purpose is to hold the authoritative state of the cooperative and to make that state legible to members, directors, officers, auditors, and automation. It is not a marketing site, a social platform, or a CRM. It is the book of record and the interface through which the cooperative operates itself.

This is version 0.2 of the PRD. Two substantive additions distinguish it from version 0.1. First, the four properties of the Ethereum Foundation Mandate (Censorship Resistance, Open Source, Privacy, Security, collectively CROPS) are made explicit as the design discipline against which architectural decisions are evaluated. Second, an evolutionary pathway names the stages through which each property migrates over the system’s phased delivery — from external check at the boundary to embodied practice in the membership.

The PRD is paired with a sibling document, Hub Operations, which specifies the operational surface the cooperative hosts under the Ethereum Everywhere grant. The two documents are deliberately layered. Hub Operations is greenfield and ships at Stage 02–03 from the first commit. The CIS is partly built and migrates progressively. The patterns established in Hub Operations flow back into the CIS as it matures past Stage 01 in the corresponding modules. The two systems share an information model, an identity layer, a treasury, and a CROPS posture.

The document is written for three audiences at once: organizers making decisions about scope and sequence, developers building on the Supabase, Make.com, and Claude API foundation, and Nou, the agent layer operating across the system on behalf of the cooperative. The voice is editorial rather than specification-heavy, because the work requires a shared understanding of intent that a rule-book cannot carry alone.

02 · Principles

These ten principles carry across every module and every interface. Where a design decision is ambiguous, the principles resolve it. Where a principle is in tension with an expedient, the principle holds and the expedient is reconsidered.

01
Augmentation, not automation
The system exists to improve human coordination, not to replace human judgment. Agents draft, surface, and guide. Humans decide, sign, and govern.
CROSS
02
Legibility is the product
A governance system that confuses its members works against the thesis that justifies its existence. Every interface must pass the test of a curious new member, not a co-op MBA.
O
03
Bylaws are the specification
The legal documents define the entities, events, thresholds, and rights the system must faithfully represent. Where bylaws are silent, board policy fills the space. Where both are silent, a deliberate product decision is required and recorded.
CROSS
04
Traceability over opacity
Every record in the system links to the authority that governs it. Every derived value links to its inputs. A member following a question back to its source should never hit an unexplained boundary.
O, S
05
Conservation at the ledger
Votes, stock, and capital accounts are conserved quantities. What enters a balance must leave another. The schema enforces this before any interface renders it.
S
06
Confidentiality at the data layer
Privacy is a property of the record, not the screen. Interfaces inherit access constraints from the underlying data, so no view can accidentally expose what the record itself protects. Article 18 obligations apply end to end.
P
07
Democratic by default
Any member can see what governs them. Aggregate figures, the membership list on request, meeting minutes, board policies, and the benefit report are all accessible without friction. Exceptions require a reason rooted in the bylaws.
O
08
Portable by construction
Data belongs to the cooperative, not the vendor. Every table has an export path. Every record resolves to an open format. Vendor lock-in at the data layer is a governance failure, regardless of tool choice.
CR
09
Audit by design
Every change to state is an event row with actor, timestamp, and prior value. The system answers “what happened, when, and why” the same way it answers “what is true now.”
S
10
Composability with other cooperatives
The schema and interfaces are designed so the model generalizes. What works for RegenHub should work for the next LCA, the next credit union, the next housing cooperative, with minimal adaptation.
O, CR
03 · CROPS as Design Discipline

Four organizational intentions, applied to one system. The CROPS Blueprint traces each property through four stages of organizational integration: from filter applied at the boundary, to operating assumption embedded in defaults, to system pattern encoded in architecture, to practice embodied in the membership. The blueprint’s honest read placed RegenHub mostly at Stage 01 across the four properties, with one Stage 03 outlier at the on-chain attestation pattern for the REA event log.

CR · Censorship Resistance
Protects against capture

No single vendor holds authoritative cooperative state. Every table has an export path. The cooperative’s institutional identity, voting record, and capital ledger persist through any single provider’s withdrawal. Critical events are optionally attested on-chain as an integrity layer, with off-chain records remaining authoritative under Subchapter K.

O · Open Source
Protects against opacity

Bylaws, policies, the patronage formula, the schema, the governing source code, and the benefit report are public artifacts. Internal deliberation remains private; the structures that govern members are open. The cooperative’s intellectual contributions accumulate as a commons that other cooperatives can adopt.

P · Privacy
Protects member agency

Member data minimization is structural. Tax identifiers, capital balances, contact details, and sensitive deliberation records are protected at the row level under Article 18. The schema enforces what policy alone cannot. Aggregate views serve governance; individual records serve the member they belong to.

S · Security
Protects against compromise

Conservation laws are enforced at the schema before any interface renders them. Every state change is an immutable event row with actor, timestamp, and prior value. The treasury is multi-sig by structure rather than by policy. The REA event log itself is a tamper-evident security artifact.

04 · CROPS Evolutionary Pathway

The four properties do not advance at the same rate. Open source is most readily defaulted to and quickest to read from outside; it is likely to reach Stage 04 first. Privacy requires the most deliberate architectural work. Security needs continuous attention to avoid oscillating back to Stage 01 under deadline pressure. Censorship resistance is most contingent on early infrastructure choices and hardest to retrofit, which is why several of its commitments must hold at Phase 01 even though the property as a whole reaches its full Stage 03 pattern only at Phase 04.

CRCensorship Resistance

Current: Stage 01 with one Stage 03 outlier · Trajectory: 01 → 02 → 02 → 03 → 03 → 04

Phase 01: Vendor selection becomes explicit. Chokepoint analysis recorded as a design artifact. Every table ships with a documented export path. Email and DNS diversified. On-chain attestation outlier preserved.

Phase 02: Capital event log built as portable, auditable structure. Tax record formats use open standards. Backup strategy survives any single vendor. Mercury treated as payment rail, not system of record.

Phase 03: Voting records optionally attestable on-chain via Base. Member-facing voting interface is open-source. Notice delivery uses multiple channels with confirmed receipt tracking. Cooperative identity becomes ENS-resolvable.

Phase 04: Member data export becomes a member right exercisable from the portal. Stock ledger and capital accounts are reconstructable from off-system records as a tested capability.

Phase 05: The walkaway test is achievable. If any single vendor disappears, authoritative state can be reconstructed and migrated within a defined recovery window.

OOpen Source

Current: Stage 02 reaching to 03 in publishing practice · Trajectory: 02 → 03 → 03 → 03 → 04 → 04

Phase 01: The schema itself is published. Agreement repository structure and signature event records documented publicly. The “build in public” reflex moves from leading-edge to default.

Phase 02: Patronage formula and capital event types publicly documented. The 704(b) compliance approach published as a reference implementation for other LCAs electing Subchapter K.

Phase 03: Voting interface code is open-source. Motion templates and decision records are public artifacts. Notice delivery system documented end to end.

Phase 04: Membership lifecycle state machine published. Redemption schedule logic open. Governance turnover and dispute mediation patterns become teachable artifacts.

Phase 05: Complete CIS reference implementation openly licensed. The cooperative’s intellectual contributions accumulate as a commons.

PPrivacy

Current: Stage 01 with Stage 03 locus at Verifiable Cities · Trajectory: 01 → 02 → 03 → 03 → 03 → 04

Phase 01: Schema-level data minimization structural from day one. Tax identifiers encrypted at rest. Row-level security scoped to roles defined in §18.1. Member directory shows only what the bylaws require.

Phase 02: Capital account visibility scoped to the member; aggregate views serve governance. Tax records encrypted with administrator-only access.

Phase 03: Vote privacy implemented where bylaws permit. Sensitive deliberations under §18.2 restricted at the row level, not the screen.

Phase 04: Member contact details portable to the member. Suspension and termination records protected even from non-administrator officers.

Phase 05: Zero-knowledge proofs available for member participation verification where appropriate. Privacy primitives are first-class architecture, not bolted on.

SSecurity

Current: Stage 01 with emerging Stage 03 patterns at treasury · Trajectory: 01 → 02 → 03 → 03 → 03 → 04

Phase 01: Threat modeling part of every design conversation. Conservation laws enforced in the database, not the interface. Audit log structural from day one.

Phase 02: Treasury is multi-sig by structure with named signers. Distribution authorization paths layered. Backup and recovery tested, not just present.

Phase 03: Vote integrity enforced at multiple layers. Motion records immutable once cast. Notice delivery has cryptographic receipt. Member account recovery has multiple paths.

Phase 04: State machine transitions signed and logged. REA event log is tamper-evident across the full lifecycle.

Phase 05: Layered defense at protocol, application, and operational levels. Key management survives any single steward’s departure.

06 · Information Model

The system’s information model follows the REA ontology (McCarthy 1982): Agents, Agreements, Events, Resources. Every table traces to one of the four primitives, and every relationship between tables is an Event. This is not a decorative choice. It is the property that keeps the accounting layer and the governance layer in the same vocabulary, and it is the structural basis for the security and audit posture defined in the pathway.

08 · Core Modules

Thirteen modules cover the cooperative’s operational surface. Eight additional modules cover Hub Operations. None is optional; each is phased, and the system is usable at every intermediate state.

Cooperative modules

Member Directory & Profile§1.7, §2.9
Canonical register of members. Personal profile, contact details, tax identifier (restricted visibility), class membership, good standing status. Supports the ten-day advance member list requirement before meetings.
Capital Account§V
Per-member capital account balance with full event history: contributions, allocations, distributions, adjustments. Tracks book versus tax distinction. Member-facing view shows personal balance; administrator view shows all.
Stock Ledger§1.13, §4.5
Patron Stock and Preferred Stock issuance, transfers back to the cooperative, redemptions. Enforces the one-per-member rule and the transfer restrictions.
Agreements Repository§1.2.8, §1.3.4, §XVI
Bylaws, Articles, board policies, membership agreements, investor documents, service contracts, the EE grant agreement, and the Hub Membership Agreement. Versioned, signed, searchable.
Meetings & Governance§II, §III
Scheduling, notice generation and delivery, agendas, attendance (in-person and electronic), minutes. Calculates quorum in real time as members join and leave.
Decisions & Votes§2.6, §2.8, §3.13–3.15
Motion capture, vote collection across modalities, threshold enforcement, outcome determination, consent workflows, referendum trigger support. All votes immutable once cast.
Notices & Communications§2.4, §X
Notice generation with legally-required timing windows, delivery tracking, waiver capture. Multiple delivery channels for censorship-resistance posture.
Benefit Report Archive§XV
Annual public benefit report generation support, publication to members and the website, historical archive. The one module with a guaranteed public view, anchoring the open source pathway.
Membership Lifecycle§1.4, §1.9, §1.10, §1.11
The state machine from application through redemption. Admission flow, withdrawal notice, suspension tracking with clock, termination hearing support, five-year redemption schedule.
Tax & Reporting§VI
K-1 preparation data, withholding calculation, fiscal year reporting, tax election tracking. Outputs feed the accountant; inputs come from the capital account ledger.
Treasury & Distributions§5.4, §5.5
Distributable Cash calculation, distribution events, payment execution through Mercury or successor banking rail, reserve policy enforcement, tax withholding on distribution. Multi-sig structure as the security pathway matures.
Onboarding§1.4, Orientation
Guided application, stock purchase, agreement signature, orientation to the bylaws and working culture. A new member should reach active status in a short, legible sequence.
Conflict & Disclosure§XI, §XVII
Director conflict disclosures, recusal tracking, dispute logging with mediation and arbitration state. Keeps the record straight when decisions are contested.

The GUI is the member’s primary surface onto the cooperative. Mobile-first, styled according to the co-op.us design system. Its job is to make the underlying state visible and actionable without requiring the member to understand the schema.

Every record detail view shows three things: the record, the governing authority (which bylaw section, policy, or grant section justifies this being a record at all), and the history of changes. A member looking at their capital account can see the balance, the clause that defines capital accounts, and every event that changed their balance.

Design system adherence: All visual elements follow the co-op.us design system — dark ground at #0f0f0f, surface panels at #1a1a1a, terracotta accent at #c4956a used sparingly, Libre Baskerville for reading and IBM Plex Mono for technical labels. The tone is editorial, grounded, and unhurried. Any member-facing screen that does not feel like a passage from a thoughtful document is off-brand.

11 · Interface — Natural Language

The natural language interface is not a chatbot bolted onto the portal. It is Nou, the agent representing the perceptive faculty of the cooperative, with the same standing as any other operational surface. Its function is to increase the collective intelligence of the member body by making the system’s knowledge legible through conversation, while operating within the same CROPS commitments as the rest of the system.

Capabilities

Question answeringgrounded
“What’s my capital account balance?” “When is the next board meeting?” “Am I in good standing?” Every answer grounded in the current state and cited to the bylaw section or record that justifies it.
Workflow guidanceprocedural
“How do I propose an amendment?” “What happens if I withdraw?” Walks the member through the bylaw-defined procedure step by step, surfacing the relevant clause at each point.
Drafting assistanceaugmentation
“Help me write a motion to amend §1.9.” “Draft minutes from today’s meeting based on the attendance and votes recorded.” The member reviews and approves before anything becomes authoritative.
Contextual surfacingproactive
Before a meeting: “You might want to review the Q2 capital account statement.” After a vote: “This outcome triggers a fourteen-day notice window.” Noticed, not pushed.
Audit supportinvestigation
“Show me every change to §5.3 since formation.” “When was the last time the board voted on a compensation matter?” Spans the record in ways that are tedious for humans and native for the agent.
Hub reportingmonthly
Drafts the monthly EE impact report from the Hub event log on the first of each month. An organizer reviews and approves before publication.

Constraints

Nou inherits the permissions of the user who invokes it. It cannot see or disclose what that user cannot. It never takes authoritative action on its own. It can prepare a motion, but the member files it. It can draft minutes, but the Secretary ratifies them. It can propose an allocation, but the board approves it. It cites sources for every substantive claim, and says “I don’t know” or “that isn’t settled” when that is the honest answer.

13 · Integration and Stack

The stack is deliberately composed of tools chosen for their fit to cooperative-scale operations: accessible to non-developers, portable in their data, interoperable through standard protocols. Each layer has a specific role and a principle governing its authority.

LayerRoleAuthority
SupabaseSystem of record. PostgreSQL with row-level security. Holds all authoritative state.authoritative
Front-endMember-facing GUI. Renders derived views, collects member input, delivers notifications. Tool choice pending.renders, does not own
Make.comEvent-driven automation. Notices, reminders, cross-system integrations, scheduled jobs.orchestrates, does not decide

XeroGeneral ledger mirror. Reference only; capital accounts remain authoritative in Supabase.reference

On-chain records do not replace the off-chain ledger. The Subchapter K election under Article VI requires that tax and accounting records remain under standard bookkeeping. Where Base carries attestations, those are confirmations of off-chain events — the censorship-resistance integrity layer named in the CR pathway — without conflating it with the security pathway, where the off-chain audit trail remains structural.

15 · Success Criteria

Success is measured along four axes. If the member axis fails, the cooperative is not democratic in practice. If the governance axis fails, the cooperative is in procedural trouble. If the compliance axis fails, the cooperative is at legal risk. If the CROPS axis fails, the cooperative has built a surface that does not honor the values it claims to serve. All four must hold.

For members

A member can find what governs any situation they are in, without asking a human. A member can see their own capital account at any time, with full history. A member can participate in any meeting through the channel they prefer, with equal standing. A member can cast a vote and see it counted, with the rule that determined the outcome cited. A member can review any historical decision that affected them.

For governance

Quorum is always correctly calculated and the underlying rule is shown. Vote thresholds are always correctly applied. Notice deadlines are always met and deliveries confirmed. Minutes are captured for every governing meeting and ratified within a reasonable window. Director and officer terms are tracked and transitions happen on schedule.

For compliance

Capital accounts balance under §704(b) at every fiscal close. Tax filings are produced on schedule with complete data. Benefit report is published annually per §XV. Record retention meets statutory requirements for each category. Any audit inquiry can be answered from the record within a reasonable time.

For CROPS

Each property has reached or exceeded its phase target on the pathway. The walkaway test is achievable at Phase 05. The published artifacts (schema, bylaws, formula, agent code) are usable by another cooperative without renegotiation. Member data minimization survives feature pressure across releases. The audit log is tamper-evident at every layer.

17 · Open Questions

These are questions the PRD cannot resolve alone. Each requires a decision from the organizer body, the board, or a specific working group. They are named here rather than smoothed over because the system cannot faithfully represent decisions that have not been made.

Patronage formula · §5.3

The draft bylaws allocate Net Profits equally. The working design is forty-thirty-twenty-ten. Until ratified, Phase 02 ships with manual allocation entry against the equal default.

Investor member framework · §1.3, §1.13.2

Preferred Stock is reserved. Term sheet, series designation, and investor documents all pending. Phase 01 schema accommodates the class; Phase 02 capital account treatment awaits the structure.

Nou’s write-capability scope · Principle 01

Read-only through Phase 03. Drafting with human approval through Phase 04. Open question: does Nou ever execute on behalf of a member with scoped, logged authorization, and under what conditions?

On-chain attestation scope · CR pathway

Which events merit public attestation: admissions, allocations, distributions, benefit reports, hub events? What is the retention model? Who controls the keys? A separate working group, with the CR pathway raising the urgency at Phase 03.

Verifiable Cities adjacency · P pathway

The Verifiable Cities use-case lab thread is the natural Stage 03 locus for the privacy property. Whether the cooperative becomes a pilot context, participating partner, or referrer is undecided.

Interoperability with sibling cooperatives · Principle 10

Cameron’s tooling at ONE Local, Start.coop’s formation work, and other cooperative builders operate adjacent surfaces. Where does the schema federate, where does it stay local?

Nou interaction retention · P pathway

Interaction logs are required for audit (principle 09) and may contain sensitive queries. Retention window, access, and redaction policy pending. The privacy pathway forces the question by Phase 03.

Non-redeemable share option · §1.9.3

Bylaws provide for five-year redemption. The organizer body has discussed a non-redeemable alternative. If adopted, Phase 04 redemption schedule logic simplifies significantly.

“A scenius has a filing cabinet, and the filing cabinet has values.”

The collective intelligence the cooperative aspires to is not a vibe or a slogan. It is the accumulation of documented decisions, visible patterns, and legible obligations that make shared context possible. The Common Information System is that filing cabinet, rendered as software.

Every member who can find what governs them, see what they contributed, and participate in what comes next is a member capable of exercising the voice the bylaws give them. The system’s job is to make that capacity universally available rather than dependent on which meetings a member happened to attend or which email threads they were on.

The CROPS discipline is what keeps the filing cabinet from becoming a vault. Censorship resistance keeps it portable. Open source keeps it inspectable. Privacy keeps it humane. Security keeps it trustworthy. The pathway names how each of those properties moves, over time, from a check at the boundary into the hands of the people who use the system. That migration is the real work, and the architecture is its scaffolding.
