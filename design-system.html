Design System v3 · adopted from co-op.us

A reference specification for the visual artifacts of a cooperative: the documents it writes to govern itself, and the instruments it builds to watch itself. This document holds the tokens, type, components, layout, and voice that make those artifacts recognizably ours, and includes the instructions an agent needs to produce them on our behalf.

First Principles

The system rests on a small number of commitments, and most of what follows is their consequence.

Terracotta is the only decorative color. Everything else lives on a warm grayscale ramp, and hierarchy is carried by weight and value rather than by hue. The accent is a condiment, not a wash: one well-placed terracotta border does more than a full panel of color.

Two faces carry the whole system. A serif for reading and a monospace for everything technical. There is no third.

State is not decoration. A separate, narrow palette exists for signaling condition, and it appears only where the work is being watched, never where it is being read.

Authority is shown, not assumed. Any figure, permission, or rule can name its source on the surface, because in this cooperative authority is event-based, time-bound, and citation-bound. A number without provenance is a rumor.

The voice is part of the design. An artifact with the right colors and the wrong voice is off-brand. The tone is grounded, unhurried, editorial, and slightly etymological. It never shouts and it never sells.

Color Tokens

The system runs in two modes, dark and light, driven by the reader's system preference with a manual override. Both modes share the same token names; components reference var(--token-name) and the active mode resolves the value. Dark is the ground state. The toggle in the header is a working example.

The core palette is the warm grayscale ramp plus the single terracotta accent. Two terracotta tokens exist because the same hue cannot both decorate and be read: --terra for borders and fills, --terra-text for type. In light mode the readable token darkens to hold contrast on parchment.

Alongside the core palette sits a narrow set of state colors, and the rule that governs them matters more than the colors themselves. They express condition: moss for what is operational or ratified, slate for what is simulated or in review, clay for what is pending or wants attention. They appear only on instrument surfaces, as chips, sparkline accents, and threshold markers. They never tint a panel, color a heading, or enter a document. Terracotta remains the only accent because these are not accents; they are signals. The trio stays inside the earth register, and there is deliberately no dashboard green and no alarm red.

Typography

Two faces, three registers. Libre Baskerville is the editorial voice: body copy, headings, anything meant to be read at length. IBM Plex Mono is the terminal voice: labels, metadata, card descriptions, and all technical content. The third register is numeric, and it is also Plex Mono.

Numbers follow accounting discipline. Every figure that someone might sum is set in mono with tabular-nums and right-aligned, so magnitudes line up vertically and the eye can compare a column at a glance. Negatives take parentheses, the accountant's convention, rather than a color, because color is reserved for state and never used to carry sign. The serif never sets a figure meant to be added.

Two Layout Grammars

Our surfaces come in two kinds, and each has its own layout. Naming both keeps a layout choice auditable rather than habitual.

The Document grammar is for reading: governance texts, briefs, this specification. A single centered column at 760 pixels, serif-dominant, with vertical scroll as the natural gesture. Sections open with a monospace label in terracotta followed by a short rule that fades from terracotta to transparent; together they are the section boundary marker.

The Instrument grammar is for watching: the live panels, the sensing surfaces, the pricing tools. Widescreen, no-scroll where the viewport allows, a twelve-column grid of tiles and panels with nested detail views in place of new pages. Mono-dominant; the serif appears only in panel titles and any embedded reading pane. Density is bounded on purpose. An instrument that needs scrolling to be understood has outgrown one screen and should split into views.

Design Principle

Terracotta is a condiment, not a wash. One well-placed accent border does more work than a full colored panel. The grayscale ramp carries the hierarchy. Vary text weight across heading, text, muted, and faint to build reading depth without chromatic noise.

The Status Chip names the condition of a thing in one mono word. The taxonomy is the cooperative's own: operational for systems doing real work, simulated for surfaces showing modeled data, ratified and in review for governance instruments, pending for anything awaiting an act of the board. Honest status labeling is a principle, not a nicety, and the chip is how an interface keeps the promise.

operational ratified simulated in review pending

The Provenance Chip is the system's signature. Provenance, from the Latin provenire, to come forth, says where a claim comes from. A figure or rule on any surface can carry its citation, a bylaw section, an event identifier, a ratification date, in a small mono chip with a terracotta left edge. It makes the deepest commitment visible: authority is event-based, time-bound, and citation-bound.

§5.3.2·patronage formula·pending ratification EVT-2026-0412·board action·2026-04-12

The Instrument Tile is the atomic unit of the instrument grammar: a faint mono label, a tabular figure, a quiet sub-line, an optional status chip. Tiles compose into panels, and panels compose into instruments.

Elevation

Three levels of depth, expressed differently by mode. In light mode depth is a soft shadow. In dark mode shadow is invisible against near-black, so depth becomes a step in surface value: a raised thing is slightly lighter, the way embers near the surface of a fire are. Components reference the level and the mode resolves the means.

Level 0page ground, --bg
Level 1cards, --surface + shadow-1
Level 2overlays, --surface-raised + shadow-2

Voice and Tone

The writing style is as much a part of the system as the palette. The tone is grounded, unhurried, editorial, and slightly etymological. It is never promotional and it does not shout. Name a thing by what it is and let the sentence rest there.

WRITE THIS

The books you co-own.

NOT THIS

Transparent financial infrastructure.

WRITE THIS

A modest start. Enough to see if the pattern holds.

NOT THIS

A revolutionary approach to cooperative finance.

On instrument surfaces the voice compresses to mono fragments, lowercase, two or three words, no verbs unless the element is a control. A control says exactly what it does and keeps its name through the whole flow, so the button that reads Publish produces the toast that reads Published.

WRITE THIS

capital accounts · aggregate

NOT THIS

Your Capital At A Glance

Forbidden patterns, without exception: em dashes, emoji, bold inside running prose, exclamation points outside direct quotes, the word leverage as a verb, the phrase at the intersection of, and buzzwords from the Web3 lexicon.

Motion and Spacing

Motion is restrained and named. Two durations cover the system: --dur-mode at 400ms for the mode crossfade, --dur-micro at 160ms for hover and focus. One easing curve, --ease. Anything slower than the crossfade or busier than a border transition needs a reason. The prefers-reduced-motion query zeroes every duration.

Spacing follows a named scale on a four-pixel base, --s1 through --s8, resolving to 4, 8, 12, 16, 24, 32, 48, and 64. Section spacing is --s8, label-to-content is --s6, card padding runs --s5 to --s6.

Accessibility

Contrast is verified in both modes. Body text exceeds AAA, and the readable terracotta token holds AA at label sizes on parchment. The state colors are tuned per mode to hold AA at chip sizes, and no condition is ever signaled by color alone: every chip pairs its color with a word and a dot. Faint text is decorative in both modes and never carries critical information. The mode toggle includes an aria-label, visible keyboard focus, and the reduced-motion override, and color-scheme is declared so form controls and scrollbars render correctly in dark mode.

Page Scaffold

Three layout grammars cover every surface in the commons. The grammar determines the navigation pattern, content width, and mode toggle placement. Choosing the wrong grammar for a surface type is the most common structural error.

GrammarUsed forNavigationMax width
IndexEntry pages, overviews.site-nav sticky bar (commons.css), in-page anchor links720px centered
InstrumentHUD, Loop, roadmap toolsCustom .topbar with back link and title; no .site-navFull-bleed
DocumentPRDs, design system, briefsInline header with title and mode toggle; no nav bar760px centered

Index grammar uses .site-nav from commons.css, a centered .page column at 720px, and a footer. The mode button sits fixed at top: 11px; right: 20px outside the page flow. Suitable for any page that needs in-page section navigation alongside cross-site links.

Instrument grammar uses a custom sticky .topbar with a back-to-commons link as the first element, a title, and the mode button inline at the right edge. The content area is full-bleed — no centered column. Instruments are full-height interactive surfaces; a traditional footer does not apply. The topbar back link is the only wayfinding required.

Document grammar uses a serif-dominant single column at 760px. An inline <header> element holds the document title and the mode toggle. Suitable for reading-oriented content — specifications, briefs, policy documents — where the reading gesture is vertical scroll.

Every page regardless of grammar must carry the mode toggle, the shared commons-mode localStorage key, and the required head block from Page Metadata. These three are grammar-invariant.

Mode toggle requirementValue
localStorage keycommons-mode — shared across all pages so the user's choice persists when navigating
Button labelShows the mode you will switch to: displays "light" when current mode is dark, "dark" when light. Never use emoji or icons.
Init orderRead commons-mode first; fall back to prefers-color-scheme; apply before first paint
html attributedata-mode="dark" on <html> — all token overrides key on this attribute

The canonical mode toggle script — three lines, no dependencies:

(function () {
  var root = document.documentElement;
  var btn  = document.getElementById('modeBtn');
  var stored = null;
  try { stored = localStorage.getItem('commons-mode'); } catch(e) {}
  var m = stored || (matchMedia('(prefers-color-scheme: light)').matches ? 'light' : 'dark');
  root.setAttribute('data-mode', m);
  btn.textContent = m === 'dark' ? 'light' : 'dark';
  btn.addEventListener('click', function () {
    var next = root.getAttribute('data-mode') === 'dark' ? 'light' : 'dark';
    root.setAttribute('data-mode', next);
    btn.textContent = next === 'dark' ? 'light' : 'dark';
    try { localStorage.setItem('commons-mode', next); } catch(e) {}
  });
})();

Footers follow the grammar. Index and document pages carry a three-element footer: repo name, GitHub link, cooperative attribution. Instrument pages carry a compact inst-footer strip — a single flex row in 10px monospace, border-top only, containing a back link, repo name, GitHub link, and attribution. Every page has a footer; the strip weight varies by grammar.

Page Metadata

Every HTML page published from this repository carries a required head block. Metadata is not decoration — it is the contract between the page and the systems that parse it: browsers, crawlers, link-unfurlers, and the sitemap. The requirements below apply to every file served under techne-co-op.github.io/commons/.

Pages divide into two categories. Public pages are indexed, canonical, Open Graph-tagged, and listed in sitemap.xml. Internal documents — working drafts, companion instruments, progressive definition records — carry noindex,nofollow and are excluded from the sitemap. The distinction is a deliberate editorial choice, not a technical afterthought. Every page still carries a description and a favicon, because those serve readers and link previews even when the page is not indexed.

The sitemap lives at sitemap.xml in the repository root and is declared in robots.txt. It lists only public pages. When a new page is promoted to public status — when its robots tag changes from noindex to index,follow — it must be added to the sitemap in the same commit. A public page not in the sitemap is an oversight, not a policy.

Page Class

Every page in the commons carries three orthogonal classification axes. These axes are independent — a page's content type does not determine its publication tier, and neither determines which nav surfaces include it. This is the key invariant: no axis implies the value of another. Composable, not inherited.

The three axes are declared as data-* attributes on the <html> element and as fields on each node in tree.manifest.json. The manifest is the authoritative record; the HTML attributes are the local declaration that CSS and JavaScript can read.
